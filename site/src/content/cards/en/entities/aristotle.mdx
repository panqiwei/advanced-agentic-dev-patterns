<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">Aristotle</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">384–322 BCE — founder of Western scientific methodology, creator of the theory of causality</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Natural philosophers before Aristotle lacked a systematic theory of causality itself. Aristotle's metaphor: they were like boxers without boxing technique — occasionally landing a good punch, but only by luck. His core methodological principle: "We have knowledge of a thing only when we have grasped its cause" — knowledge is causal knowledge, and understanding means being able to answer "why".</CoreDef>
</Band>

      <span class="kt-text">Physics II 9</span>

    <div class="kt-row kt-pa">
      <span class="kt-text">Parts of Animals I</span>
      <span class="kt-cont">Explanatory priority of final cause over efficient cause — the "man begets man" argument</span>

  <div class="legacy">
    <Kicker>Methodological legacy</Kicker>
    <KV label="Non-psychological teleology">The final cause excludes any craftsman's desire — teleological explanation applies to natural processes without anthropomorphism</KV>
    <KV label="Four-cause framework">Identifies four irreducible kinds of explanation, raising causality from practical tool to theoretical framework</KV>
    <KV label="Link to Herbert Simon">Both offer frameworks for explaining complex systems — Simon decomposes by structure, Aristotle by explanatory role</KV>
