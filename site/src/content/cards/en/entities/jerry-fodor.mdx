<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">Jerry A. Fodor</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">Philosopher and cognitive scientist (Rutgers / MIT) — founder of the language-of-thought hypothesis and the systematicity argument</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Fodor was among the most influential philosophers working in cognitive science. His 1988 co-authored paper with Pylyshyn critiquing connectionism is one of the most widely cited in the field — the core argument: connectionism cannot explain the systematicity of human cognition, because distributed representations lack the syntactic constituent structure that compositionality requires. This directly triggered the formation of the neurosymbolic AI research program.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="major-works">
    <Kicker>Three core contributions</Kicker>
    <div class="mw-row mw-loth">
      <span class="mw-year">1975</span>
      <span class="mw-name">Language-of-Thought Hypothesis (LOTH)</span>
      <span class="mw-desc">Thought is computation over syntactically structured symbolic expressions — a "mental language," Mentalese</span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-row mw-mod">
      <span class="mw-year">1983</span>
      <span class="mw-name">Modularity of Mind</span>
      <span class="mw-desc">Cognition comprises relatively independent domain-specific modules; central systems are isotropic and unencapsulated</span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-row mw-conn">
      <span class="mw-year">1988</span>
      <span class="mw-name">Connectionism critique (with Pylyshyn)</span>
      <span class="mw-desc">"Any architecture that makes systematicity a contingency is the wrong architecture" — 40 years later LLMs supplied an empirical answer</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="legacy">
    <Kicker>Theoretical legacy</Kicker>
    <KV label="Triggered neurosymbolic research">Smolensky's subsymbolic-level theory is a direct reply to Fodor & Pylyshyn</KV>
    <KV label="Dhar & Søgaard 2024">40 years on, LLM evidence: scale boosts systematicity, but instruction tuning reduces it — partially vindicating Fodor's prediction</KV>
  </div>
