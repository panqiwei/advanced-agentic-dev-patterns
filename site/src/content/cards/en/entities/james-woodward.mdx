<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">James Woodward</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">Philosopher of science at the University of Pittsburgh — principal architect of the interventionist theory of causation</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>In Making Things Happen (2003), Woodward laid out a systematic interventionist framework: X has a direct causal effect on Y iff there is an intervention on X that, holding other variables fixed, changes Y. Its key property — non-reductive but non-circular: defining X–Y influence only invokes relations among variables other than X and Y. Compared with counterfactual semantics, interventionist semantics track scientific practice more closely.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="theory">
    <Kicker>Core interventionist framework</Kicker>
    <KV label="Four conditions on an intervention variable">A precise characterization of a "surgical" intervention — affects Y only via X, not through any other path</KV>
    <KV label="Non-reductive, non-circular">Defining X–Y influence via influence among other variables yields an acyclic network of definitions</KV>
    <KV label="Type-level focus">Primarily analyzes the general relation "X influences Y" — well suited to causal reasoning in scientific practice</KV>
    <KV label="Modularity (with Hausman)">Each structural equation can be independently broken by intervention — guaranteeing the feasibility and locality of interventions</KV>
  </div>
  <div class="relations">
    <Kicker>Relations to other theorists</Kicker>
    <KV label="Parallel to Pearl">Independently developed an intervention-based formal framework, strongly complementary to Pearl's do-calculus</KV>
    <KV label="Alternative to Lewis">Interventionist semantics replaces Lewis's possible-world semantics — closer to the operational concepts of experimental science</KV>
  </div>
</Band>
