<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">Nelson Goodman</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">American philosopher (1906–1998) — moved the induction problem from "is it justified?" to "which inductions are justified?"</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>In Fact, Fiction, and Forecast (1955), Goodman posed the grue problem: the same body of observations can support mutually contradictory inductive conclusions — differing only in which predicate is used. "Green" is projectible, "grue" is not. This "New Riddle of Induction" shows Hume never explained why we project some predicates and not others.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>

    <div class="grue-def">
      <span class="gd-label">Definition of Grue</span>
      <span class="gd-text">Something is grue iff: it is examined before 2100 and is green, or is examined after 2100 and is blue</span>
    </div>
    <div class="grue-paradox">

      <div class="gp-row gp-bad">

  <div class="works">
    <Kicker>Main Works and Responses</Kicker>
    <KV label="Fact, Fiction and Forecast (1955)">Introduced the grue problem — the core paradox of inductive logic</KV>
    <KV label="Entrenchment Reply">Projectibility comes from the history of linguistic practice, not objective structure of the world — "green" became entrenched through frequent use</KV>
    <KV label="Ways of Worldmaking (1978)">Constructivist worldview — the world is a plurality of versions constructed by our symbol systems</KV>
    <KV label="AI Connection">An LLM's projection patterns are determined by the language distribution of its training data, not external reality — Goodman's entrenchment theory explains this</KV>
  </div>
