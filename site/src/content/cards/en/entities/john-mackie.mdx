<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">J. L. Mackie</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">Australian-born British philosopher (1917–1981) — proposer of the INUS-condition analysis</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>In "Causes and Conditions" (1965), Mackie first introduced the INUS analysis: what we ordinarily call a "cause" is an Insufficient but Non-redundant part of an Unnecessary but Sufficient condition for the effect. This is the most influential technical contribution within the regularity-theory tradition and remains an important reference frame for legal causal reasoning.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="inus-detail">

    <div class="inu-row inu-i">
      <span class="inu-letter">I</span>
      <span class="inu-full">Insufficient</span>
      <span class="inu-desc">Not enough on its own to bring about the effect — X is not a sufficient condition</span>

    <div class="inu-row inu-n">
      <span class="inu-letter">N</span>
      <span class="inu-full">Non-redundant</span>
      <span class="inu-desc">Indispensable within the sufficient condition it belongs to — X is non-redundant</span>
    </div>
    <div class="inu-row inu-u">
      <span class="inu-letter">U</span>
      <span class="inu-full">Unnecessary</span>
      <span class="inu-desc">The sufficient condition it belongs to is not itself necessary — other paths can bring about the effect</span>
    </div>
    <div class="inu-row inu-s">
      <span class="inu-letter">S</span>
      <span class="inu-full">Sufficient</span>
      <span class="inu-desc">The sufficient condition, as a whole, suffices for the effect</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="other-works">
    <Kicker>Other contributions</Kicker>

    <KV label="Moral error theory">Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong (1977) — moral judgements are systematically false because they presuppose nonexistent objective moral properties</KV>
    <KV label="Heir to Hume">Directly extends and develops Hume's analysis of causation — a pivotal link in the regularity-theory tradition</KV>
