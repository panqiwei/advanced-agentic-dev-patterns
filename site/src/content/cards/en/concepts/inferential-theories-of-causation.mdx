<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">Inferential Theories of Causation</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">Inferential Theories of Causation — C causes E iff E is inferable from C</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Heir to the regularity tradition: the mark of causation is not constant conjunction of event types but an inferential path from cause to effect — through appropriate background knowledge (laws, facts) and inference rules. Directionality rides on the inferential path.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="theories">
    <Kicker>Four major inferential theories</Kicker>
    <div class="th-row">
      <span class="th-name">DN model (Hempel 1948)</span>
      <span class="th-desc">Effect logically derived from laws + antecedent. Symmetry problem: tower height ↔ shadow length, both derivable</span>
    </div>
    <div class="th-row">
      <span class="th-name">Ranking functions (Spohn 2012)</span>
      <span class="th-desc">Ranks of disbelief as epistemic states. C is a cause of E iff C raises E's epistemic status (conditionalization)</span>
    </div>
    <div class="th-row highlight">
      <span class="th-name">Strengthened Ramsey test</span>
      <span class="th-desc">Deductive non-redundancy rules out spurious causes: laws on a genuine inferential path cannot be derived from more basic laws</span>
    </div>
    <div class="th-row">
      <span class="th-name">Kairetic account (Strevens)</span>
      <span class="th-desc">Every element of the explanatory core makes a difference to the effect — difference-making + inferential unified</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="advantages">
    <KV label="Over regularity">Inferential relations can be non-monotonic (allow exceptions); carry direction; distinguish basic from derived laws</KV>
    <KV label="Handles singular causation">Even without a matching universal generalization, a rich background theory supports singular inference (dinosaur extinction)</KV>
  </div>
</Band>
