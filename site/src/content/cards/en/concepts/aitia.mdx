<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Aitia carries both "cause" and "explanation" — not a translation ambiguity, but a conceptual tension Aristotle deliberately preserves. Cause is explanation; explanation answers the "why" (dia ti).</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="aitia-grid">
    <div class="aitia-col">
      <Kicker>Metaphysical side</Kicker>
      <p>Causes are actual explanatory relations among things in the world — some features of nature really do explain others</p>
    </div>
    <div class="aitia-col">
      <Kicker>Epistemological side</Kicker>
      <p>"We only have knowledge of a thing when we have grasped its cause" (APst. I 2) — to understand is to grasp the cause</p>
    </div>
    <div class="aitia-col">
      <Kicker>Causal pluralism</Kicker>
      <p>One thing can have more than one non-accidental cause — an ontological commitment, not a concession to incomplete knowledge</p>
    </div>
    <div class="aitia-col">
      <Kicker>Explanatory standard</Kicker>
      <p>Per-se (kath'hauton) vs accidental — "the daughter of X made the bronze statue" is true, yet does not explain</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-sunk" dense>
  <p class="aristotle-note">Earlier philosophers "like untrained boxers — landing a pretty punch now and then, but only by luck": they <em>used</em> causation without <em>understanding</em> it.</p>
</Band>
