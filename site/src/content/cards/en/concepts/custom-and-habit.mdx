<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Habit is not some newly discovered principle of mind — it just is the general name for the associative principle. Repeated experience forms a propensity; propensity makes us expect; expectation produces belief. Reason cannot justify causal inference, yet we do it anyway — what drives it is habit.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <Quote cite="Hume EHU 5.1.5">Whenever the repetition of any particular act or operation produces a propensity to renew the same act or operation… we always say, that this propensity is the effect of custom.</Quote>
  <div class="habit-roles">
    <div class="hr-item">
      <span class="hr-label">Basis of causal inference</span>
      <p>Constant conjunction → habit → propensity → expectation → causal belief. Not a rational proof, but a natural propensity.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="hr-item">
      <span class="hr-label">Source of the uniformity principle</span>
      <p>The belief that "the future will resemble the past" is itself a product of habit — not a rational premise</p>
    </div>
    <div class="hr-item">
      <span class="hr-label">Source of belief's force</span>
      <p>The vivacity of a present impression passes along associative pathways to the idea of the effect, giving it "the firmness of belief"</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-sunk" dense>
  <p class="darwin-note">Hume: "To depend on custom rather than reason is better — nature's ordinary wisdom secures survival." This anticipates Darwin: habit is a cognitive mechanism selected for by evolution, not a second-rate substitute for reason.</p>
</Band>
