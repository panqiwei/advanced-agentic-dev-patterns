<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">Grue Problem</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">New Riddle of Induction — from "can we induce" to "how do we induce"</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Grue = observed before time t and green, or observed after t and blue. Same emerald observations + same inductive pattern: "green" → predicts green; "grue" → predicts blue. When induction is valid, which predicates earn the right to be projected?</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="paradox-table">
    <div class="pt-row pt-header">
      <span>Predicate</span><span>Evidence</span><span>Prediction after t</span>
    </div>
    <div class="pt-row green">
      <span class="pt-pred">green</span>
      <span>All emeralds pre-t are green</span>
      <span class="pt-result green-r">green ✓</span>
    </div>
    <div class="pt-row grue">
      <span class="pt-pred">grue</span>
      <span>All emeralds pre-t are grue</span>
      <span class="pt-result grue-r">blue ✗</span>
    </div>
    <p class="pt-note">Same data, same reasoning pattern, contradictory conclusions</p>
  </div>
  <div class="projectibility">
    <Kicker>Projectibility</Kicker>
    <KV label="Goodman's answer">Entrenchment — predicates frequently used in past successful inductions are more projectible</KV>
    <KV label="Controversy">This grounds the justification of induction in linguistic convention rather than objective world structure</KV>
  </div>
  <div class="ml-note">
    <span class="ml-label">ML mapping</span>
    <p>Overfitting = learning a grue-like predicate — perfect on the training segment, broken after deployment. Time-dependent distribution shift is exactly the grue signature.</p>
  </div>
</Band>
