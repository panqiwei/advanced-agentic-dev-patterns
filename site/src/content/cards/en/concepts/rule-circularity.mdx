<Band tone="paper-raised" dense>
  <h2 class="card-title">Rule Circularity</h2>
  <p class="card-subtitle">Rule Circularity — using the very inference rule under scrutiny to argue that that rule is reliable</p>
</Band>

<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>Rule circularity: argue that inference rule R is reliable using R itself. Distinct from premise circularity (conclusion smuggled into the premises): in rule circularity the premises are genuinely true, but the inference relies on the rule being defended. The debate: is rule circularity vicious?</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <div class="two-views">
    <Kicker>Two positions</Kicker>
    <div class="tv-row view1">
      <span class="tv-pos">Rule circularity is acceptable</span>
      <span class="tv-arg">Deduction faces the same problem: Lewis Carroll (1895) showed you cannot persuade someone who rejects modus ponens to adopt it. Foundational inference rules have nowhere to retreat to — "all we can reasonably demand is that they endorse themselves" (Lange 2011)</span>
    </div>
    <div class="tv-row view2">
      <span class="tv-pos">Rule circularity has serious trouble</span>
      <span class="tv-arg">Counter-induction (CI: most A are B → most A are not B) can also justify itself! If induction is allowed a rule-circular defense, bad rules get one too — which severely weakens the defense</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="responses">
    <Kicker>Two response strategies</Kicker>
    <KV label="Popper falsificationism">Dodge induction to dodge rule circularity — science only needs falsification, not inductive justification</KV>
    <KV label="Meta-induction (Schurz)">Bring in independent justification at the meta level — wMI's reliability can be proved without depending on the inductive rule itself</KV>
  </div>
</Band>
