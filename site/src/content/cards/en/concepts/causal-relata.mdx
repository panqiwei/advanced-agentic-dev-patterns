<Band tone="tan">
  <CoreDef>What kind of entity is a cause, and what kind is an effect? The answer to this foundational question shapes every commitment a causal theory makes. The "causal-difference argument" keeps pressing: c causes but c* does not → they must be different entities.</CoreDef>
</Band>

<Band tone="paper-raised" grow>
  <PillarGrid cols={2}>
    <Pillar label="Events">Most popular — but individuation is contested: coarse-grained (Quine) / Davidson's causal individuation / Kim's fine-grained (property+object+time) / Lewis's property account</Pillar>
    <Pillar label="Facts">Handle absences/omissions as causes — "Anna didn't water the plant" isn't an event but can be a fact; opponents deny absences are genuine causes</Pillar>
    <Pillar label="Variable values">The modeling tradition: Hitchcock/Woodward. The point isn't the single value, but how it's packed into a variable — same Kim-style triple, different variable, different role</Pillar>
    <Pillar label="Contrastivism">Causation is a four-place relation: c rather than c* causes e rather than e*. Focus/stress fixes the implicit contrasts, no need to distinguish the relata themselves</Pillar>
  </PillarGrid>
</Band>
